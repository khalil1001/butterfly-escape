SF, The place to be?

We have all seen San Francisco and its streets at least once in Hollywood movies. It’s mainly known for its iconic Golden Gate Bridge and its proximity to the Silicon Valley. Neck to neck with New York City, it has one of the most expensive lifestyles in the world.

Well, from my own perspective, with no doubt, San Francisco is full of opportunities, but it’s also an amazing place where it feels good to live and to explore… I think I can’t describe San Francisco without romanticizing it a bit : its awesome natural beauty, unique landmarks and open-hearted lifestyle make it so magical and unique. A vibrant city with clement weather, surrounded by national parks and ocean cliffs.. Too much beauty in one place! 😍

« SF » or « The City », is at the edge of the American continent, overlooking the Pacific Ocean, in northern California, aka the Golden State, not far from the Hawaiian islands, and crossed by the fog or « Karl », as locally named. It somehow has its little own world, separate in geography and in spirit from the rest of America and the world. Despite its rich history of inclusion, tragedy and resilience, it remains brilliant and innovative. And above all, authentic and true to herself. To understand what I mean by that, let’s take a look back at its history. 

A brief History

It has the story of seismic change on the ground and in its people and mindset. Here are 7 historical keys to understand its evolution. 

Gold Rush 

It was originally called Yerba Buena under Mexican rule, before joining the USA in 1848, just as the Gold Rush changed the city forever. It was then the centre of finance, culture and commerce for America’s west.

1906 earthquake 

In 1906, a major earthquake struck the city and Northern California. It was devastating , causing widespread destruction and fires that ravaged much of the city. The quake and subsequent fires resulted in the deaths of an estimated 3,000 people and left around 250,000 homeless.

1930s Rebound 

After the earthquake devastation and the stock market crush in 1929, SF started reinventing itself again and experienced a significant rebound in the 1930s, marked by a wave of construction and modernization like building the Golden Gate and Bay bridges. The architectural styles of this era reflected a blend of Art Deco and modernist influences, contributing to the city’s distinctive skyline and revitalizing its economy during the Great Depression.

Beat Generation

The Beat Generation emerged in the 1950s as a literary and cultural movement centered around a group of writers and artists who rejected mainstream values. It flourished in the North Beach neighborhood. The City Lights Bookstore became a hub for Beat writers and their works, known for their spontaneous writing style, jazz influence, and a countercultural lifestyle that embraced nonconformity.

Summer of Love & the 1970s

In early 1967, the Summer of Love began first at the Human Be-In in Golden Gate park. Then, many thousands of ‘flower children’ descended the Haight-Ashbury neighborhood in the summer of 1967. In the 1970s, the city became a centre of the Gay rights movement with the emergence of the Castro neighborhood. 

Dot-com boom

With the dot-com boom since the late 1990s to early 2000s, driven by the rise of internet, technology companies and the new startup culture, Silicon Valley became the capital of innovation and entrepreneurship. Many tech firms moved into the city, resulting in rising real estate prices and a shift in the local economy. The boom ended with the dot-com crash in 2000, leading to economic challenges. Despite this, SF saved its reputation as a tech hub.

AI revolution

Today’s generative AI revolution began with the founding of OpenAI in SF in 2015. In the past few years, the city has become the global epicenter of AI. A civic pride campaign was recently launched by a group of business leaders with the slogan « It All Starts Here, SF ». 

City of contrasts 

Visiting SF in 2024 is a genuine joy for curious travelers. Exploring its hoods made me realize how unique and complex the city is.

Landscapes diversity

The stunning natural beauty and steep 50 hills contrasts with its skyline and bustling streets.

Social disparities

Wealthy areas like Pacific Heights contrasts with other neighborhoods like Tenderloin and highlights significant social challenges.

Cable Car VS Self-driving cars

Cable cars were invented in SF in 1873, and are still in operation today. Jumping into the Cable Car contrasts with taking a ride with Self-driving car (Waymo). These are unique experiences you can only try in SF.

Architecture Diversity

Due to diverse cultures and communities, SF shows an open sky museum and a glimpse of each Architecture, below some that you can’t skip.

  • Buildings of the downtown skyline
  • Victorian houses « Painted Ladies »
  • Chinatown « Dragon Gate »
  • Japanese temple « Peace Pagoda »
  • Russian Orthodox Cathedral « Holy Virgin »
